Automation and audit over gates and approvals.

Gates assume people misbehave unless blocked; audit assumes good faith and lets the record keep everyone honest.

The shift: from prevention to flow.

A gate is a friction point: a human in the loop who must approve before work proceeds. The gate slows the work and produces an inbox full of decisions that no one can later audit. The default posture becomes stop until told otherwise.

Audit inverts the posture. The default is flow, and the record keeps everyone accountable: every change and every decision is captured in Git history. The record supplies the accountability, so the work keeps moving.

Gates
An email in an inbox. A human bottleneck. An unauditable approval. Default posture: stop.
Audit
Git history and merged pull requests. Every decision traceable. Default posture: flow.

Gates don't vanish. They become policy as code.

The gate becomes an automated, auditable check that runs in CI, and the result lands in the repository's history alongside the change it judged.

.github/workflows/audit.yml 
on: [pull_request]
jobs:
  audit:
    steps:
      - run: docassert validate documents/**/*.md
  consistency:
    steps:
      - run: docassert consistency   # broken traces block the merge

Where gates still belong.

Some contexts legally require a person's signature, and the framework accommodates them. Human approvals don't disappear; they become the documented exception rather than the default.

When a human signature is required
Model it explicitly: branch protection that requires a named reviewer on the pull request. The approval lands in the merge record with the rest of the history. One of the signals is now a person's explicit sign-off, and the trail is the same trail.
